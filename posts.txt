Date: 16/03/2026
Category: Imaginaries
Tags: Violence and harm, Community, Safety, Acceptance, Intersectionality
Content: Queer justice feels like it could be two possible things: justice for queer people or justice by queer people. Or I suppose it could be both. And I think when we are talking about justice, it's also about what requires justice. Is it the harm done to LGBTIQA+ communities--physical or emotional? Is it about fighting erasure and working towards recognition and representation? <br> Justice to me evokes something reactionary--something was done to us, so now we respond. But I think it can and has to be more proactive than that. Many marginalised communities have had to make their own justice, carve out their own spaces, and build solidarity. Queer justice can be about joy. It's about holding my girlfriend's hand in public. It's about celebrating pride. Queer justice is in the novels I read with ace and aro protagonists and the pride flag I hang in my window. Queer justice is sometimes quiet and sometimes loud. It is hopeful and it is hard sometimes to hold onto.  <br> But queer justice is also about struggle--the same struggle that I think is not only queer in nature. Queer justice is about standing with other marginalised communities. Queer justice is about supporting each other, protecting each other, and fighting battles together. Queer justice can't be achieved without disability justice, and First Nations justice, and migrant justice, and worker justice. Queer justice cannot be divorced from the housing crisis, the cost of living crisis, the underfunding of social and community services, the declining literacy rates of children and adults, the threat of climate change. Queer justice isn't one thing, it's actually a lot of small things, all connected. I think when we are talking about queer justice, we have to think about who we are bringing with us in this journey for justice. Because if we are leaving people behind then it is not justice at all.

Category: Futures
Tags: Acceptance, Community, Education, Law and the courts, Safety
Content: A just future in my mind looks just for everyone, or as just as can be. It's when two people can hold hands outside without thinking about what people may think. Justice can be enacted by communities by speaking out against those who use hateful language, but also educating them and building community. A just future is as much about connection and respect, as it is about recognition, and changing the status quo.
